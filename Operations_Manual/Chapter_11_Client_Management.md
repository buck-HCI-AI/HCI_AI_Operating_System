## 11.1 Overview

HCI clients pay $3–10M to build their dream home in Aspen. They are intelligent, high-performing people who are not accustomed to being surprised. The HCI client management standard is simple: they should never hear bad news for the first time. They should hear it from Buck, with a plan, before they have to ask.

Every client touchpoint is a reflection of HCI's brand. The AI system manages the data. Buck and the PM manage the relationship.

---

## 11.2 Client Communication Hierarchy

**For all client communication:**
1. PM prepares the draft (using approved language and templates)
2. Buck reviews and approves
3. PM sends from Buck's Outlook account (or Buck sends directly)

**No AI sends client communications autonomously.** GBT can draft; only Buck approves and sends.

**Communication channels in priority order:**
1. In-person meeting or phone call (for significant news, decisions, or relationship moments)
2. Buck's personal email from Outlook (for formal project communications)
3. Houzz messaging (for routine project updates logged in the system)

**Never use:**
- Personal text messages for business commitments (no written record)
- Generic construction app messages unless they're logged in Houzz

---

## 11.3 Client Communication Cadence

**Weekly:** Progress update email — what happened this week, what's next week, any pending client decisions.

**Monthly:** Budget and schedule update — formal report with numbers. Routed through Buck.

**As-needed:** Change order approvals, design decision requests, significant project events.

**At milestones:** Brief note when major construction milestones are reached (foundation poured, framing topped out, drywall complete, etc.). Clients love milestone photos.

## 11.5 How to Deliver Difficult News

**The four components of a difficult project update:**
1. **Acknowledge the situation directly.** Don't bury it in a page of good news.
2. **Explain the cause clearly and factually.** No jargon, no contractor-blaming.
3. **State what HCI has done and is doing about it.** Clients can handle problems if someone owns them.
4. **Give a specific path forward with dates.** "We expect to resolve this by [date]."

**Example — budget overrun notification:**
```
"I want to be upfront with you before our next meeting. As we've completed more of the 
subcontract awards, the projected cost is coming in higher than our original contract 
value. I've done a full budget review and want to walk you through the numbers in person. 
I'm available Tuesday at 2pm or Wednesday morning — when works for you?"
```

**Not this:**
```
"The subs came in higher than expected. We're working on it."
```

## 11.6 Change Order Communication with Clients

**Every change order is a client conversation before it's a document.**

**The sequence:**
1. Buck calls the client to explain what changed, why, and the estimated cost/schedule impact
2. Client says they want to proceed
3. A formal Change Order Authorization (COA) is prepared by GBT
4. Buck and the client sign the COA
5. Then and only then does HCI issue a Change Order to the sub

**Never:**
- Issue a CO to a sub before the client has signed the COA
- Present a CO surprise in a pay application
- Tell a client a CO "will probably be around $X" without the formal backup

## 11.7 Client Decisions and Selection Management

**Client selections are a schedule driver.** If a client hasn't selected their tile by the time the tile sub needs to order, the project stops.

**The selections tracking approach:**
1. Interior designer maintains the selections schedule
2. PM tracks which selections are needed and by when in the project schedule
3. Anything with a lead time > 6 weeks is flagged 8 weeks before it's needed
4. PM notifies the design team and the client when a selection is at risk

**What to do when a client is slow on selections:**
1. One reminder in writing (email) from the PM to the design team
2. If no response in 5 days: Buck calls the client directly
3. Buck documents the impact: "If we don't receive your [item] selection by [date], it will push the [milestone] by [X] days."
4. That documentation protects HCI on schedule claims if the client later complains about the timeline

## 11.8 Pre-Construction Meeting with the Client

Before construction starts, HCI holds a pre-construction meeting with the client. This sets expectations and prevents surprises.

**Meeting agenda:**
1. Introductions — key team members (Buck, PM, superintendent, key design team)
2. Project summary — scope, contract value, schedule overview
3. Communication protocol — how will we communicate? How often? Who is the primary contact?
4. Selections schedule — what decisions need to be made and by when?
5. Site protocol — when can the owner visit the site? Who should they contact?
6. Change order process — how changes are handled, authorization required
7. Payment schedule — when invoices go out, payment due dates
8. Q&A

**Meeting is documented.** Key decisions and agreements from the meeting go into the system.
